The film feel very akin to Frankenstein to me

---

That read is well grounded. The film is effectively a late-industrial, bio-engineered reframing of the same core myth: a human “maker” produces life through technique rather than procreation, treats it as an object, and then discovers that the created being is not a closed system but a moral agent with claims on its creator.


The Tyrell–Roy encounter is the closest structural analogue to the Victor–Creature confrontation in the novel. In both works, the created being has done the existential math: it did not choose to exist, it has been built with constraints, and the only person with authority to answer for those constraints is the maker. The demand is not merely technical (“extend lifespan”) but ethical (“account for what you made, and what you withheld”). The request for “more life” maps cleanly to the Creature’s demand for relief from a condition imposed at manufacture: not a random tragedy, but a design decision.


The “Frankenstein feel” also comes from shared thematic circuitry. There is the hubris frame (creation as mastery, not stewardship), the abandonment frame (the maker’s emotional distance from consequences), and the knowledge-is-not-wisdom frame (high capability paired with low moral responsibility). Even the rhetoric of “revision” versus “irreversibility” plays like Shelley’s warning about thresholds: once you cross into creation, you cannot unmake the obligation by appealing to technical limitation.


Where the film modernizes Shelley is in institutionalizing the maker. Frankenstein is a lone auteur-scientist; Tyrell is a system actor inside an economy, so “life” becomes a product category with planned obsolescence, and moral evasion becomes corporate process rather than private shame. That shift matters: it makes the tragedy less about one man’s terror and more about a culture’s incentives, where the created being’s suffering is not an accident but an engineered externality.


If you want to push the Frankenstein resonance even harder when you discuss the film, the cleanest lens is “creation without care.” The horror is not that life is made; it is that life is made and then treated as disposable, while still being human enough to ask why.

You are pointing at a core Scott-era tension: “machine” can mean a living, analog system that computes through chemistry, or a designed, digital system that computes through symbols. Frankenstein, Blade Runner, and Alien keep swapping which one is framed as the more frightening machine, and that swap is where the philosophical voltage lives.


In Alien, the organism is analog in the strongest sense. Its “program” is not written as code; it is embodied as a life cycle. It does not need explanation, ideology, or language to execute; it simply runs. The ship, the company, the crew’s procedures are all human-scale “digital” structures (rules, protocols, chains of command) that collapse under a substrate that does not care about those abstractions. The horror is that the analog machine is indifferent, and that indifference outperforms human systems design. “Perfect organism” is basically “perfect analog algorithm”: robust, self-propagating, and optimized for hostile environments.


Blade Runner complicates the binary by making the “synthetic” beings biological in substrate but “digital” in governance. Replicants are wetware bodies, but they are treated like software releases: versioned models, spec sheets, warranties, planned obsolescence, decommissioning. The digital layer here is not the body; it is the control regime. Roy’s “more life” lands as a demand to break the product constraint that was imposed not by biology but by design authority. The dread comes from watching personhood forced to live inside a product lifecycle.


Frankenstein sits between them and shows the earlier, gothic version of the same split. The Creature is assembled rather than born, which reads “synthetic,” but once animated he is analog: he learns, suffers, adapts, and becomes irreducible to the maker’s intent. Victor believes he is doing something like “engineering,” but he discovers he has created a living system whose emergent behavior cannot be debugged. That is the moment the digital fantasy (control through design) is defeated by the analog reality (life as runaway complexity).


Across all three, the machine question becomes: where is the control surface, and who holds it? In Alien, control sits with the organism’s lifecycle and the corporation’s incentives; humans are a transport layer. In Blade Runner, control sits with corporate design constraints (lifespan, classification) and policing; the “machine” is as much bureaucracy as biology. In Frankenstein, control is imagined to sit with the lone maker, until it catastrophically doesn’t.


A clean way to articulate your analog–digital insight is to say that these stories are not really about “is it alive or is it artificial,” but about “can life be rendered legible enough to be managed like code.” Alien answers no because analog life is too indifferent and too efficient. Blade Runner answers no because even if you can manufacture the substrate, you cannot manufacture moral containment; the created subject exceeds the governance model. Frankenstein answers no because creation without stewardship produces emergence, and emergence is the enemy of control.


If you want to turn this into a reusable critical lens for writing or development work, treat each work as a different failure mode of “digitalizing the analog.” Alien is the failure of containment. Blade Runner is the failure of commodification. Frankenstein is the failure of authorship. That trio gives you a durable scaffold for comparing scenes, characters, and institutions without reducing everything to “man vs monster.”

2. Scoring mechanics (deterministic)


2.1 Clause score contribution


Each clause contributes to each mode it carries. There are two reasonable defaults; pick one and keep it invariant within v1.


export CLAUSE_ALLOCATION_RULE="split"   # or "full"


Definition:


Split allocation (recommended)
If a clause has k modes, each mode gets weight(mode) / k.

Full allocation
If a clause has k modes, each mode gets weight(mode) (more aggressive; inflates multi-tag clauses).


Given your current tagging density, “split” keeps vectors comparable across scenes.


2.2 Scene raw vector


For a scene with clauses c ∈ C, raw score for mode m:


$$
S_{\text{raw}}(m)=\sum_{c\in C,, m\in modes(c)} \frac{w(m)}{k(c)}
$$


where w(m) is the mode weight, and k(c) is number of modes on clause c (only in split rule).


2.3 Normalized vector (0..1 per scene)


Normalize by the scene’s total mass so the vector is interpretable as proportions:


$$
S_{\text{norm}}(m)=\frac{S_{\text{raw}}(m)}{\sum_{j} S_{\text{raw}}(j)}
$$

def clause_mode_mass(mode_weight: float, k_modes: int, allocation_rule: str) -> float:
    if allocation_rule == "split":
        return mode_weight / k_modes
    if allocation_rule == "full":
        return mode_weight
    raise ValueError("Unknown allocation_rule")

def score_scene(clauses):
    # clauses: list of dicts with keys: modes, clause_importance(optional)
    raw = {}
    for c in clauses:
        modes = c["modes"]
        k = len(modes)
        I = c.get("clause_importance", DEFAULT_CLAUSE_IMPORTANCE)
        for m in modes:
            raw[m] = raw.get(m, 0.0) + I * clause_mode_mass(MODE_WEIGHT_MAP[m], k, CLAUSE_ALLOCATION_RULE)

3. Scoring mechanics with uncertainty (deterministic)


Define per-clause base mass for a mode:


$$
B(c,m)=I(c)\cdot \frac{w(m)}{k(c)}
$$


where
I(c) is $CLAUSE_IMPORTANCE,
w(m) is $MODE_WEIGHT_MAP[m],
k(c) is number of modes tagged on the clause (for split; if full, use k(c)=1).


3.1 Expected raw score (mean)


$$
\mu_m=\sum_{c: m\in modes(c)} p(c)\cdot B(c,m)
$$


where p(c) is $CLAUSE_CONFIDENCE.


3.2 Raw score variance (SD driver)


Under $UNCERTAINTY_MODEL="bernoulli_tag_correct", treat each clause-tag contribution as “present” with probability p(c):


$$
\sigma_m^2=\sum_{c: m\in modes(c)} p(c)\cdot(1-p(c))\cdot B(c,m)^2
$$


This gives you scene_raw_sd[m] = sqrt(σ_m^2).


3.3 Normalized scene vector (mean)


Let total raw mass be  $T=\sum_m \mu_m$ . Normalized mean:


$$
\bar{v}_m=\frac{\mu_m}{T}
$$


3.4 Normalized SD (approximate)


Normalization couples modes (covariance). v3 uses a pragmatic delta-method approximation:


Assume  $Cov(\mu_m, T)\approx \sigma_m^2$  and  $Var(T)=\sum_j \sigma_j^2$ .


$$
Var(\bar{v}_m)\approx \frac{\sigma_m^2}{T^2}+\frac{\mu_m^2\cdot Var(T)}{T^4}-\frac{2\mu_m\cdot Cov(\mu_m,T)}{T^3}
$$


Then scene_norm_sd[m] = sqrt(Var(v̄_m)).


3.5 Index mean and SD


Index mean is the sum of normalized means for modes in the index:


$$
\bar{I}X=\sum{m\in Modes(X)} \bar{v}_m
$$


Index SD (approx) ignores covariance between modes (conservative tuning later is possible):


$$
SD(I_X)\approx \sqrt{\sum_{m\in Modes(X)} Var(\bar{v}_m)}
$$



4. Reference implementation pseudocode (v3)


def score_scene_v3(clauses, mode_weight_map, indices, allocation_rule="split",
                   default_importance=1.0, default_confidence=1.0):
    mu = {}          # raw mean per mode
    var = {}         # raw variance per mode

    for c in clauses:
        modes = c["modes"]
        k = len(modes) if allocation_rule == "split" else 1
        I = c.get("clause_importance", default_importance)
        p = c.get("clause_confidence", default_confidence)

        for m in modes:
            base = I * mode_weight_map[m] / k
            mu[m] = mu.get(m, 0.0) + p * base
            var[m] = var.get(m, 0.0) + (p * (1.0 - p) * (base ** 2))

    T = sum(mu.values()) or 1.0
    V = {m: mu[m] / T for m in mu}

    varT = sum(var.values())
    varV = {}
    for m in mu:
        cov_m_T = var[m]  # approximation
        vp = (var[m] / (T**2)) + ((mu[m]**2) * varT / (T**4)) - (2 * mu[m] * cov_m_T / (T**3))
        varV[m] = max(vp, 0.0)

    index_mean = {ix: sum(V.get(m, 0.0) for m in ms) for ix, ms in indices.items()}
    index_sd = {ix: (sum(varV.get(m, 0.0) for m in ms) ** 0.5) for ix, ms in indices.items()}

    return {
        "scene_raw_mean": mu,
        "scene_raw_sd": {m: var[m] ** 0.5 for m in var},
        "scene_norm_mean": V,
        "scene_norm_sd": {m: varV[m] ** 0.5 for m in varV},
        "index_mean": index_mean,
        "index_sd": index_sd,
        "total_mass_mean": T
    }

Reference pseudocode:


def score_scene_v3_2_mc(clauses, mode_weight_map, mode_conf_map, indices, n=20000, seed=123):
    rng = Random(seed)
    samples = []
    for _ in range(n):
        raw = {}
        for c in clauses:
            I = c.get("clause_importance", 1.0)
            p = c.get("clause_confidence", 1.0)
            if rng.random() > p:
                continue
            modes = c["modes"]
            k = len(modes)
            for m in modes:
                w = mode_weight_map[m] * mode_conf_map.get(m, 1.0)
                raw[m] = raw.get(m, 0.0) + I * (w / k)
        T = sum(raw.values()) or 1.0
        norm = {m: raw[m]/T for m in raw}
        idx = {ix: sum(norm.get(m,0.0) for m in ms) for ix,ms in indices.items()}
        samples.append((norm, idx))
    return summarize(samples)  # mean, sd, percentiles
